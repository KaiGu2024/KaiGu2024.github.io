AI-Text-Detection — Three Periods

Where the field stood before Liang et al., what they contributed, and what has happened since.

1 · Before Liang et al.
~2019–2023

  • Fine-tuned BERT / RoBERTa classifiers. Train a transformer on labeled (human-text, AI-text) pairs; one forward pass per document outputs a probability.
  • Zero-shot statistical probes. Use a reference LM as a judge — no labeled data. Score text by perplexity, by the rank of each token under the LM, or by how much its log-probability drops under small perturbations (DetectGPT-style curvature).

Effectiveness: ~95–99% AUROC on the clean, in-distribution benchmarks each paper used (e.g., GPT-2/GPT-3 essays, news). But OpenAI withdrew its own classifier in July 2023 citing “low rate of accuracy”; per-document detectors collapse under paraphrase, on short texts, on non-native English, and out of domain.

2 · This article
Liang et al., ICML 2024

  • Distributional MLE — estimate the corpus-level fraction $\alpha$ of AI-modified content; do not classify individual documents.
  • Vocabulary $V$: adjectives only — topic-confound minimized; most stable across years.
  • Set-of-tokens trick: length-sublinear penalty makes the likelihood tractable.
  • Reference $Q$: ChatGPT prompted with the historical review instructions for the same papers — matched conditioning.

Reported gain (Appendix D): BERT-classifier baseline yields 6.2% / 11.2% corpus-level estimation error (in-distribution / OOD); Liang et al.'s MLE yields 1.8% / 2.4% — a 3.4–4.6× reduction, plus 7 orders of magnitude faster than per-document detection.

3 · After this article
2025–2026

  • HACo-Det (Su et al., ACL 2025) · aclanthology.org/2025.acl-long.1069
    • How it works: Drops the document-level binary label and labels every word human or AI instead. Builds a benchmark of co-authored documents where a human writes some sentences and an LLM writes others. Trains a token-level classifier (transformer + per-token head, like NER) to output a per-token AI probability; sentences are then classified by majority vote on their tokens.
    • Performance: Finetuned token classifiers substantially outperform retrofitted document-level detectors (DetectGPT-family, GPTZero, etc.) on sentence-level F1 in the realistic mixed-authorship setting.

⚠ Why per-document classification fails — and corpus-level estimation doesn't.

Per-document detection asks “is this document AI?” — a binary decision on every document. Three failure modes:

  • Errors compound. 1% FPR × 1M documents = 10,000 wrongful flags. “95% accurate” is unusable at audit scale.
  • Per-document signal is weak. Reviews are short (~200 words), in many styles, often by non-native English speakers — where detectors are already biased.
  • Adversaries win. Light edits or paraphrase destroy the per-document signal almost entirely (Saha & Feizi 2025).

Corpus-level estimation asks “what fraction involves AI?” — a continuous parameter $\hat\alpha$ with standard errors. Three things flip:

  • Bias is removable, variance shrinks. Per-doc errors enter a single estimate; the bias is calibratable from a labeled validation set, and the standard error shrinks as $1/\sqrt{N}$ — more documents make the estimate more precise, not more wrong.
  • Signal aggregates. A tiny adjective-frequency shift per document becomes a strong, statistically significant shift across millions of tokens.
  • Adversary cost rises. A paraphraser can hide AI in one document, but cannot rewrite every document in a way that preserves the corpus-wide vocabulary distribution.

The 95% “in-distribution benchmark” numbers measure document-level accuracy on a clean test set — the wrong target. Liang et al. switch from classification to estimation:

$$\hat\alpha \;=\; \arg\max_{\alpha}\;\sum_{i}\log\!\big[(1-\alpha)\,P(x_i) + \alpha\,Q(x_i)\big]$$
  • $P(x_i)$ — likelihood of document $x_i$ under the human reference distribution (estimated from pre-ChatGPT reviews).
  • $Q(x_i)$ — likelihood under the AI reference (ChatGPT prompted with the same review instructions on the same papers).
  • $\hat\alpha \in [0,1]$ — estimated fraction of the corpus drawn from $Q$; i.e., the share of reviews substantively LLM-modified. $\hat\alpha = 0$ means all-human, $\hat\alpha = 1$ means all-AI.

Figure 2. Build human/AI reference corpora → estimate $\hat p, \hat q$ on adjectives → MLE on the target corpus.

What changed across the three periods

  • Unit of analysis shifted: document (Period 1) → corpus (Period 2) → sub-document segment (Period 3).
  • Adversary model hardened: clean text → light edits → an attacker that automates paraphrase-to-evade.
  • Per-document classification is no longer credible as a stand-alone audit tool — the Saha & Feizi attack effectively closes that era.
  • Distributional thinking — introduced by this paper — is now the default frame for population-scale audits across science (Kobak 2025 is the canonical follow-up).

Robustness — Proofreading Is Not Enough

Figure 5. Apply ChatGPT to only fix grammar/typos in real reviews; compare to real-corpus dashed lines.

Test design

  • Take real human reviews; have ChatGPT fix only grammar/spelling.
  • Re-run the estimator on the proofread corpus.

Result

$\hat\alpha$ rises only slightly — far below the post-ChatGPT real-corpus levels (dashed lines: ICLR 10.8, NeurIPS 9.1, CoRL 6.5).

★ Read. The method picks up substantial modification, not surface editing.

Robustness — Outline Expansion

Figure 6. Two-stage: human bullet outline → ChatGPT expands → mix at known $\alpha$.

The realistic workflow

Reviewer drafts a bullet outline → LLM expands into prose. The MLE recovers ground-truth $\alpha$ accurately across $\alpha\in\{0,.05,\ldots,.25\}$.

Three modes of LLM use, and what each does to $\hat\alpha$:

ModeReviewerLLM$\hat\alpha$
ProofreadingFull draftPolishes EnglishUnder-detects
Outline expansionBullet outlineExpands to proseDetects
Scratch generationPrompt onlyWrites everything$\hat\alpha \to 1$
⚠ The favored interpretation. If reality were all proofreading, the observed $\hat\alpha = 10.6\%$ (ICLR 2024) would be impossible. If it were all scratch generation, $\hat\alpha$ would be 50–80%, not 10%. Outline expansion is the middle case whose simulated $\hat\alpha$ matches reality — so the authors read the headline numbers as: ~10% of reviews involved substantial LLM expansion of human outlines, not 10% wholly AI-written. The substantive judgments stay human; the prose, framing, and rhetorical structure are largely AI. Behavioral correlates (deadline-proximity ↑ $\hat\alpha$, rebuttal-engagement ↓ $\hat\alpha$) reinforce this reading.

Behavioral — Homogenization

Figure 10. Reviews closest to the embedding centroid (most "convergent") have higher $\hat\alpha$ than the most divergent reviews, at every conference.

The corpus-level monoculture signal

  • "Convergent" = small embedding distance to the corpus centroid.
  • Convergent reviews are 2× more AI-like at ICLR / NeurIPS / EMNLP.
  • LLM use compresses the diversity of feedback authors receive.
★ Punchline. Helpful at the individual level + corrosive at the corpus level. The empirical complement to Messeri & Crockett's "monoculture of knowing" warning (s08).

Takeaways & Discussion

Three takeaways

  1. Distributional > instance-level for any aggregate question about LLM presence in a corpus — orders of magnitude faster and more accurate than per-document classifiers.
  2. The post-ChatGPT shift in AI-conference reviews is real (~6.5–16.9%) and is concentrated in time-pressed, low-confidence, low-engagement reviews.
  3. Homogenization is the deeper risk — reviews are converging in style, compressing the diversity of expert feedback. Helpful individually, corrosive collectively.

Discussion

  1. Methodological shelf-life. $P$ is fixed at pre-Nov-2022 text. As humans absorb AI-style vocabulary, $\hat\alpha$ will increasingly mix genuine LLM use with stylistic contagion. Fine for now; worth re-baselining in replications a few years out.
  2. Complementary metrics for AI's direct impact. $\hat\alpha$ measures presence, not effect. Useful complements: information-to-noise ratio, depth/breadth per unit length, logical coherence — these surface where AI assistance adds value.
  3. AI adoption as quasi-exogenous framing variation. LLMs neutralize gendered or evaluative wording. The Nov-2022 shock is a discontinuity — e.g., DiD on whether LLM-mediated course descriptions narrow gender gaps in major applications, and similar designs wherever wording moves behavior.
