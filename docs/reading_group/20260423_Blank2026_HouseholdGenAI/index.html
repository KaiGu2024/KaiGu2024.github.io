Outline

Research question: How does ChatGPT adoption reshape U.S. household time allocation between productive and leisure online activities — and how large are the implied non-market productivity gains?

Who adopted ChatGPT?
Adoption rates, timing, and the GenAI Divide — high-income and young households adopt faster with no sign of convergence.
Causal traffic reallocation
IV estimates show ChatGPT adoption shifts browsing from productive to leisure sites; productive time is statistically and economically unchanged.
ChatGPT usage context
ChatGPT is used overwhelmingly alongside productive tasks (80% of GPT-window browsing); the leisure increase is a downstream consequence of efficiency gains.
Quantifying productivity gains
Structural model with Engel elasticities implies a 76%–176% efficiency gain on productive digital tasks — 2–4× larger than workplace experiment estimates.

Data & Setting

Comscore Internet Browsing Panel
  • Continuous tracking panel of U.S. desktop/laptop machines, 2021–2024; benchmarking period Jan–Dec 2021
  • Every visit logged: URL, timestamp, duration, session ID
  • Demographics: income (8 bins), age (11 bins), MSA (Metropolitan Statistical Area — Census-defined metro regions mapped via BLS QCEW county crosswalk)
  • Comscore pre-processing: drops visits <3 sec; 30-min inactivity timeout; multi-layer bot/fraud detection
  • Mobile excluded — likely underestimates total GenAI use

LLM Website Classification (Appendix A)
  • Scope: top 160,000 domains by 2023 page views (>95% of internet use); duplicates removed
  • Metadata: Beautiful Soup scrape of each domain's HTML title, description, keyword tags
  • Model: GPT-4.1 Mini (OpenAI, May 2025) via Azure — prompted to produce a 2–3 sentence description + top-5 user activities per domain
  • Classification dimensions (two-step):
    • Usage type: Productive (work, education, health, civic…) / Leisure (gaming, social, streaming…) / Ad platform/CDN
    • Usage diversity: Single-use (pure productive or leisure) / Mixed-use (both important)
  • Validation: two authors manually labeled top 70 domains; prompt iteratively refined until LLM and manual labels agreed; cross-checked via ATUS activity embeddings + cosine similarity (very similar labels)
Concerns on Step 3 (domain-level LLM description):
  1. Scale vs. accuracy: Running GPT-4.1 Mini on 160K domains is expensive and likely error-prone at the long tail where domains are obscure or have sparse metadata — LLM may hallucinate descriptions for low-traffic sites with no usable meta tags.
  2. Domain granularity too coarse: Different URL patterns within the same domain serve very different functions. E.g., reddit.com/r/MachineLearning (research, productive) vs. reddit.com/r/gaming (leisure) would both inherit reddit.com's single domain-level label. Annotating the top ~10K URL patterns would likely cover >90% of traffic with substantially more accurate labels.
  3. Step 6 redundancy: The instrument Ej is the count of exposed activities out of 5, built directly from Step 5. A separate website-level exposure call (Step 6) adds no information that Step 5 aggregation cannot provide. The paper gives no justification for this step — the appendix subsection opens directly with "Here is the prompt used…" with no rationale.
Classification Examples (Table 2 — Top 5 domains by category)
Doubts on classification outcomes:
  1. Amazon.com → Productive: The paper follows Aguiar & Hurst (2013, JPE), which uses the ATUS time-use framework where shopping is classified as non-market household production (alongside cooking and cleaning) — not leisure. On that logic, Amazon = productive task. But much Amazon browsing is recreational (wish-list browsing, deal-hunting, entertainment products); the ATUS rationale does not obviously transfer from physical shopping trips to open-ended online browsing.
  2. Google/Bing → Productive, Yahoo → Mixed: Defensible in principle — Google and Bing are designed as task-oriented search engines, while Yahoo evolved into a portal blending news, sports, entertainment, and games. Yet the line is arbitrary: Google is also heavily used for leisure searches, and the distinction hinges on the LLM reading the automated description ("widely used search engine" vs. "comprehensive web portal") rather than actual usage.

Identification & Estimation

1. Endogeneity Problem

ChatGPT adoption is correlated with life events that independently shift browsing — OLS conflates the causal effect with selection.

Example 1: Job loss → household uses ChatGPT to draft cover letters and increases job-search / online education browsing — OLS attributes the latter to ChatGPT.
Example 2: Workers in GenAI-exposed occupations are introduced to ChatGPT at work and also differ in skills/productivity → browsing reflects occupation, not ChatGPT effect.
2. Instrument: Household GenAI Exposure (measured in 2021)
\[ \text{HHGenAIExp}_i = \sum_j \phi_{ij} \cdot \mathbf{1}\!\left[E_j \in \{4,5\}\right] \]
  • \(\phi_{ij}\): share of household \(i\)'s browsing duration on site \(j\), measured Jan–Dec 2021
  • \(E_j \in \{0,\ldots,5\}\): count of activities on site \(j\) substitutable by ChatGPT
  • Threshold \(E_j \in \{4,5\}\): switching costs mean households need high substitutability to adopt
  • Intuition: a Bartik-style index — pre-existing "need" for ChatGPT based on precise 2021 task mix
Instrument relevance: adoption rate by exposure tercile
3. Exclusion Restriction & Controls

Conditional on demographics and controls, variation in HHGenAIExp reflects only precise task-level differences in 2021 browsing — these are implausibly related to 2024 life-event shocks.

  • Age-bin × income-bin × MSA fixed effects — absorb all observable demographic and regional differences
  • Bartik share control — \[ \text{BartikShare}_i = \sum_j \phi_{ij} \cdot \mathbf{1}\!\left[E_j \geq 1\right] \] Partialling out \(\text{BartikShare}_i\) absorbs the general tendency to browse any ChatGPT-exposed site; the residual identifying variation is then purely whether the household was unusually concentrated on the most substitutable sites (\(E_j \in \{4,5\}\)), conditional on its overall exposure footprint. Analogous to controlling for total industry employment share in a standard Bartik design.
  • 3-year gap — 2021 task mix predicts 2024 adoption, making short-run confounds implausible
Discussion: Is the exclusion restriction credible? What stories could violate it, and do the controls adequately address them?
4. Long-Difference IV (2024 vs. 2022)

Why long difference? Adoption was minimal before 2024; a 2-year window smooths noise and captures sustained change.

2nd stage
\[ \Delta \text{Outcome}_i = \gamma \cdot \text{ChatGPTUse}_i + \lambda_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{FE}} + X_i'\xi + \varepsilon_i \]
1st stage
\[ \text{ChatGPTUse}_i = \theta \cdot \ln(\text{HHGenAIExp}_i) + \lambda_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{FE}} + X_i'\xi + u_i \]
KP F-stat = 104  ·  N = 42,886  ·  SE clustered at age × income × MSA
First-stage dynamics: effect of ln(HHGenAIExp) on ChatGPT adoption by quarter — justifies long difference at 2024
Pre-2022Q4 coefficients are mechanically zero — no one had adopted ChatGPT yet, so the DV = 0 for all households. These zeros carry no information about the exclusion restriction.

Model — Household Time Allocation  (Aguiar et al. 2021)

Impact of GenAI on Household Production
Interpretation: A ChatGPT efficiency shock (ξz ↑) shifts the household's productive time budget outward. Because productive digital tasks behave as a time necessity (ηz < 1), the household reaches the same output level with less time on z — freeing time that is reallocated to leisure ℓ, a time luxury (ηℓ > 1). The diagram maps directly to the IV finding: Δ log leisure = +1.512**, Δ log productive = 0.
Setup
  • Household allocates digital time: leisure ℓ and productive tasks z, with z + ℓ ≤ 1
  • Isoelastic utility curvature ηa:
      ηa > 1: time luxury — more efficiency → more time
      ηa < 1: time necessity — more efficiency → less time
  • ChatGPT = efficiency shock to productive tasks only (ξz ↑)
Key prediction: ηz < 1 → efficiency gain ↓ time on z, ↑ time on ℓ  ✓  matches Table VI
Engel elasticity estimates (Table IX, IV)

Instrument: local precipitation shocks (discourage outdoor activity → ↑ total browsing time)

Takeaways & Discussion

Three takeaways
  1. GenAI reshapes home life, not just work. Households use ChatGPT mainly for productive tasks; the payoff is more leisure. Non-market productivity matters for welfare accounting.
  2. Efficiency gains at home exceed the workplace. 76%–176% vs. 25%–56% in experiments. Why? Novice users + multi-site browsing collapse into a single query.
  3. A growing AI Divide. Young and high-income households adopt faster; the gap is widening with no sign of convergence through 2024.
Discussion questions
  1. Is the exclusion restriction credible? What stories could violate it, and do the pre-trend tests and controls adequately address them?
  2. Is "productive vs. leisure" browsing the right welfare-relevant distinction? Could the same pattern reflect lower welfare?
  3. Why are home efficiency gains 2–4× larger than workplace estimates?
  4. Does a widening adoption gap necessarily imply growing welfare inequality?
  5. How would including mobile usage change the picture — in magnitude and direction?
